Din Tai Fung
鼎泰豐 — Da'an, Taipei
The restaurant that made soup dumplings (xiao long bao) world-famous. Started as a cooking oil shop in 1972, now a global empire — but this is the original. Each dumpling is hand-folded with exactly 18 pleats around a thin skin filled with pork and hot broth. Bite, slurp the soup, repeat. Order: Pork XLB (NT$105/5), truffle XLB (NT$450/5), chicken soup. Cash only. Go before 11:30am or after 2pm. Dongmen MRT Exit 5.

Yongkang Beef Noodle
永康牛肉麵 — Da'an, Taipei
Beef noodle soup is Taiwan's unofficial national dish — rich, spiced broth with thick wheat noodles and fork-tender braised beef chunks. This spot has been the benchmark for decades and holds a Michelin Bib Gourmand. Sichuan-style spicy broth is the move. Order: Braised beef in spicy broth (NT$230), add steamed spare ribs. 11am-8:30pm. Cash only. Dongmen MRT, 10 min walk.
Bib Gourmand Map →
Fuhang Soy Milk
阜杭豆漿 — Zhongzheng, Taipei
Taiwanese breakfast is a whole meal culture — not cereal, not pastries, but savory egg crepes (dan bing), flaky sesame flatbread (shaobing) stuffed with fried dough sticks (youtiao), and warm soy milk served sweet or savory with vinegar and chili. This place, hidden on the 2nd floor of Huashan Market, is the most famous breakfast shop in all of Taiwan. Opens 5:30am, closed Mon. Weekday before 7am = 20 min wait. Weekend = 1hr+. Cash only.
Breakfast Map →
Jin Feng Lu Rou Fan
金峰魯肉飯 — Zhongzheng, Taipei
Lu rou fan — braised pork rice — is the dish every Taiwanese person has an opinion about. Finely minced pork belly slow-braised in soy sauce, five-spice, and fried shallots, ladled over steaming white rice. It costs almost nothing and tastes like home. This no-frills shop near CKS Memorial is considered the best in Taipei. Large bowl NT$50, stewed egg NT$15. Total meal ~$3 USD. Open 8am-1am. Cash only.
Street food Map →
Fuzhou Pepper Pork Bun
福州世祖胡椒餅 — Raohe, Taipei
A hu jiao bing (pepper pork bun) is a fist-sized dough ball stuffed with black pepper-marinated pork and scallions, then slapped onto the inside wall of a blazing charcoal clay oven — like a tandoor. The outside blisters crispy while the inside stays juicy. This stall at the entrance of Raohe Night Market is the most famous in Taiwan. ~NT$60 each. Always a 20-30 min queue — watch them make it while you wait.

A-Mei Tea House
阿妹茶樓 — Jiufen
A multi-story wooden teahouse draped in red lanterns, clinging to a mountainside above the Pacific Ocean. Widely believed to be the building that inspired the bathhouse in Miyazaki's Spirited Away. You sit on tatami, a teapot of high-mountain oolong is brewed for you, and you look out over the ocean while eating traditional sweets. Tea + sweets set NT$300. Go at dusk when the lanterns light up. Weekends packed — teahouses across the street have similar views with fewer people.
Tea Map →
Chun Shui Tang
春水堂 — Taichung
This is where bubble tea was born. In 1983, a staff member at this Taichung teahouse poured sweetened tapioca balls into cold milk tea on a whim — and created one of the most popular drinks on Earth. This is the original shop, still serving the original recipe. The tea is made from real loose-leaf tea, not powder. Order: Original Pearl Milk Tea (NT$70-140). Go mid-afternoon to skip crowds. 20 min walk from Taichung TRA station.
Tea / Origin Map →
Du Xiao Yue
度小月 — Tainan
Tainan is Taiwan's food capital, and this is its most iconic restaurant — serving the same dan zai noodle recipe since 1895. A small bowl of thin noodles in a rich shrimp-and-pork broth, topped with minced meat, a shrimp, and a boiled egg. Intentionally tiny because it was designed as a snack between meals. The name means "slack season noodles" — a fisherman invented it to make money when the sea was too rough. Sit at the counter to watch them cook.

Addiction Aquatic Development
上引水產 — Zhongshan, Taipei
Imagine Tsukiji fish market crossed with a high-end food hall. A massive converted warehouse with live seafood tanks, a 12-meter sushi counter run by a Japanese head chef, charcoal grills, hot pot stations, and a gourmet supermarket. You can pick your own live fish and have it prepared on the spot, or grab stunning sashimi platters for a fraction of Tokyo prices. 7am-10pm. Cash only (!). Eat takeaway at Rongxing Park nearby.
Seafood market Map →
Ningxia Night Market
寧夏夜市 — Datong, Taipei
Taiwan's night markets are open-air food festivals that happen every single night. This one is the locals' favorite — compact, no-nonsense, almost entirely food stalls. None of the souvenir junk, just serious eating. Multiple Michelin Bib Gourmand stalls here. Try the lu rou fan, oyster omelets, and taro ball soup. Near historic Dihua Street — combine the two for a great evening.
Night market Map →
Miaokou Night Market
廟口夜市 — Keelung
A night market built around a temple in Taiwan's biggest port city. The stalls have been numbered for decades — locals refer to them by number, not name. The seafood comes straight off the boats docked meters away. Famous for: crab thick soup (stall #5), ding bian cuo (a Fujianese noodle soup unique to Keelung), shrimp tempura, and peanut ice cream rolls wrapped in a crepe. 30 min from Taipei by train.
Night market Map →
Shilin Night Market
士林夜市 — Shilin, Taipei
The biggest and most famous night market in Taiwan — a sprawling maze of food stalls, game booths, and shops. The underground food court is where the action is: XXL fried chicken cutlets bigger than your head, crispy stinky tofu, oyster omelets, and massive papaya milk shakes. Yes it's touristy. Still worth one visit, especially if it's your first night market. Jiantan MRT, open nightly.
Night market Map →
A Hsing Milkfish Congee
阿星鹹粥 — Tainan
Milkfish (shi mu yu) is Tainan's signature fish — a silvery, bony fish farmed in the region's coastal ponds for centuries. At this Bib Gourmand shop (open since 1965), it's served as congee: rice porridge with chunks of milkfish, topped with fried garlic. Ask for the belly cut — it's the fattiest, silkiest part. Queue starts at 5am because this is what Tainan locals eat for breakfast. Air-conditioned inside.
Bib Gourmand Map →
Ice Monster
ICE MONSTER — Da'an, Taipei
Taiwanese shaved ice is nothing like a snow cone. Instead of shaving plain ice and adding syrup, they freeze the fruit itself into blocks and shave THAT — so every ribbon of ice is pure mango flavor. This shop pioneered the technique and serves towering bowls with fresh mango chunks, mango ice cream, and condensed milk. Named one of the world's best dessert shops. Best in mango season (May-Oct).
Dessert Map →
Hayashi Department Store
林百貨 — Tainan
Taiwan's first department store with an elevator, built in 1932 during the Japanese era. Bombed in WWII — you can still see bullet holes and shrapnel scars on the exterior walls. Beautifully restored and now houses local artisan crafts, Tainan-made souvenirs, and a rooftop cafe with views of the old city. The elevator still works. A gorgeous place to rest, shop, and absorb Tainan's layered history.

Beautiful Places

Taiwan's scenery is wildly underrated
Tap the places you'd want to see. These are the "I can't believe Taiwan looks like this" moments.
Taroko Gorge
One of the world's only river-carved gorges cut through solid marble. 1,000m canyon walls, turquoise rivers, and trails carved into the cliff face. Taiwan's most iconic natural wonder. Note: partially closed since 2024 earthquake — check trail status.
Qingshui Cliffs
A 21km stretch of sheer cliff dropping 1,000m straight into the Pacific. The water below shifts between three distinct shades of blue. Considered one of Taiwan's 8 natural wonders — you drive along a highway carved into the cliff face. Just north of Taroko.
Alishan Sunrise
A narrow-gauge forest railway built over 100 years ago climbs through bamboo and ancient cypress forest to 2,500m. At dawn, you take a pre-sunrise train to a viewpoint and watch golden light break over a sea of clouds. One of Taiwan's "Five Wonders." Requires a 4:30am alarm.
Sun Moon Lake at Dawn
Taiwan's largest lake, nestled in a mountain basin at 750m elevation. Named because the east side is shaped like a sun and the west like a crescent moon. The 30km cycling loop around it is ranked among the world's best bike rides. Misty at dawn, stunning at sunset. Indigenous Thao tribe culture here.
Jiufen at Dusk
A former gold mining town clinging to a mountainside, now famous for its narrow stone stairways lined with hundreds of red lanterns and traditional teahouses. Said to have inspired the town in Spirited Away. During the day it's a crowded tourist street; at dusk, when the lanterns glow against the ocean view, it transforms into something magical. Go late.
Yehliu Geopark
A coastal peninsula where millions of years of wind and wave erosion carved sandstone into surreal formations — mushroom rocks, honeycombed pillars, and the famous "Queen's Head" (a rock that naturally looks like an Egyptian queen in profile). It's an alien landscape that doesn't look like it belongs on Earth. 1 hour from Taipei.
Gaomei Wetlands
A vast tidal flat near Taichung where the sky reflects perfectly in thin sheets of water at low tide, creating an infinite mirror effect. At sunset, the entire landscape turns gold. You walk out on a long wooden boardwalk over the wetland. One of Taiwan's most photographed spots — but timing is everything. Go at sunset during low tide.
Xiao Liuqiu
A tiny coral island off Taiwan's southwest coast with crystal-clear water and one of the highest densities of sea turtles in the world. You snorkel right off the beach and green sea turtles swim alongside you — no boat needed. The whole island is scooter-sized, no cars. 30 min ferry from the mainland. Taiwan's best-kept secret.
Elephant Mountain
A short (20 min) but steep jungle trail that ends at a rocky viewpoint directly facing Taipei 101 and the city skyline. THE classic Taipei photo spot. At sunset, 101 glows orange against the mountains; at night, the city lights up below you. Free, no reservation, accessible by MRT (Xiangshan station). Go 45 min before sunset.
Beitou Hot Springs
A volcanic hot spring valley just 30 minutes from downtown Taipei by MRT — no car needed. Sulfurous steam rises from a turquoise creek, and century-old Japanese-era bathhouses line the valley. You can soak in public baths for a few dollars or book a private room. Taipei's easiest half-day escape. The Thermal Valley viewpoint (free) has bright-green boiling water.
Alishan sunrise requires a 5am train. Gaomei Wetlands are magical only at sunset. Jiufen lanterns glow at dusk. Are you willing to plan around the light?
